# Agentic-AI Model Risk Management for Financial Institutions

## Overview

On April 17, 2026 the Federal Reserve (SR 26-2), the OCC (Bulletin 2026-13), and the FDIC jointly issued revised interagency *Guidance on Model Risk Management* — the long-awaited modernization of the 2011 SR 11-7 / OCC Bulletin 2011-12 framework. In a move that surprised most of the bank-MRM practitioner community, the agencies explicitly placed **generative AI and agentic AI outside the scope of the new guidance**, on the published rationale that the technology class is "novel and rapidly evolving." The agencies signaled a forthcoming **interagency request for information (RFI) addressing model risk management generally and considering, in particular, banks' use of AI, including generative AI, agentic AI, and AI-based models** — anticipated as the formal vehicle for collecting public input ahead of any future rulemaking or supplementary guidance specific to the new technology class.

The result is a **governance gap** that every regulated financial institution operating Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, or any other generative or agentic system must navigate without prescriptive supervisory text — while still being held to the broad supervisory expectation that "a banking organization's risk management and governance practices should guide the determination of appropriate governance and controls for any tools, processes, or systems not covered" by the new guidance. This KB note frames the gap, the practical posture financial institutions are converging on, and how the gap maps to KRASA AI-touching skills.

It is adjacent to but distinct from `agentic-payments-kya-governance.md` (which covers the payments-protocol-layer KYA + mandate + token + audit-trail discipline) and `governed-data-agentic-layer.md` (which covers the MCP-or-equivalent layer between system-of-record and the agent runtime). This note covers the **model-risk** discipline that sits over the top of both — the supervisory framework that asks *whether the firm understands, measures, monitors, validates, and controls the AI model itself*.

## Why This Matters Now

Three signals collide in the second quarter of 2026:

**Regulatory exclusion is intentional, not accidental.** The interagency text is explicit that gen AI and agentic AI are out of scope by design — not because the agencies do not see the risk, but because the agencies are unwilling to commit to prescriptive text on a technology class that is still maturing. The forthcoming RFI is the supervisory signal that the gap is temporary; the agencies expect institutions to govern the technology under existing risk-management principles in the interim. The September 2024 Treasury report on AI in financial services, the OCC's bank supervision priorities through 2025–2026, the FRB's earlier SR 11-7 framework, and the SEC's 2026 Examination Priorities (AI / automated-tools focus, including the "AI washing" prong) collectively imply the institution's standard: build the governance now, document it under existing controls, and be ready for the RFI-driven refinement.

**Practitioner consensus is forming around the SR 11-7 spine plus AI-specific overlays.** The NIST AI Risk Management Framework (RMF), the FS-ISAC FS-AI-RMF crosswalk, the FFIEC IT Examination Handbook, OCC heightened-standards guidance, and the FRB's existing model-risk capital framework remain the de facto reference set. Practitioner publications (KPMG, Deloitte, Databricks, Lumenova, Archer IRM, Risktemplate.com) have already published crosswalks that map AI-system characteristics into SR 11-7's three-pillar structure (development; implementation and use; ongoing monitoring and validation). The financial-services industry view is that an institution which extends SR 11-7 discipline to AI systems will be substantially compliant when the RFI-driven supplementary text arrives.

**The May 2026 Anthropic ten-agent reference release, the Fiserv agentOS launch, the FIS Financial Crimes AI Agent, the Allvue + RSM Capital Operating Model, the OneStream Finance Agentic Layer, the OpenAI + PwC Native Finance Function, and the SAP Autonomous Finance Suite all ship into the gap.** Every one of these surfaces ultimately runs as a model (or composition of models) that is making decisions, drafting outputs, screening cases, or executing on behalf of the institution. Each surface is in scope for the institution's MRM discipline regardless of whether the supervisory text formally extends to it. The exclusion does not mean the technology is unsupervised; it means the institution carries the burden of demonstrating it has applied risk management proportional to the inherent risk.

## The Practical Posture

The bank-MRM and RIA-MRM communities are converging on six elements that an institution operating gen AI or agentic AI in scope-relevant workflows should have documented and operating in the interim:

**Inventory and tiering.** Every gen AI / agentic AI surface in use is registered in the model inventory. Each entry carries: the vendor (or "in-house"), the model family and version, the use case, the in-scope process, the principal user community, the data-classification level the model touches, the materiality tier (per institution-specific tiering criteria — typically a function of dollar exposure, customer impact, regulatory-decision linkage, and irreversibility-of-action), the human-in-the-loop posture (drafts / decisions / actions), and the model-owner / business-sponsor pair. The inventory should treat each composition of agent + skill bundle + connector set + subagent topology as a distinct entry if the composition produces a distinct decision surface (so the same underlying Claude Opus serving a pitch-builder workflow and a KYC-screener workflow is two inventory entries, not one).

**Risk assessment proportional to tier.** Higher-tier surfaces (customer-facing decisions, regulator-facing outputs, SAR-narrative drafting, sanctions screening, credit decisioning, AML disposition, model-prediction-driven trade execution, agent-initiated payment release) receive heavier development, validation, and monitoring discipline. Lower-tier surfaces (internal research, drafting of human-reviewed materials, summary of well-bounded inputs) receive lighter but still documented discipline. The tier drives the depth of testing, the governance-board reporting cadence, the change-control rigor, and the documentation expectation. Two skills with the same architecture but different output-use cases will sit in different tiers.

**Development discipline.** Each skill / prompt / agent definition is treated as model code: version-controlled, code-reviewed, accompanied by a model-development document describing the design rationale, the input boundary, the output boundary, the known failure modes, the tested edge cases, the model-selection rationale, and the alternative approaches considered. Skill files are stored in the same kind of repository discipline a credit-scoring model's source would be — not in shared drives or email threads. The Anthropic May 2026 reference architecture (skills as version-controlled Markdown, connectors as governed access, subagents as composable units) is itself a development-discipline scaffold the institution can adopt.

**Implementation and use controls.** Role-based access is enforced from the governed-data layer (see `governed-data-agentic-layer.md`) so that the model's permissions inherit from the principal's identity, not from the model itself. Human-in-the-loop checkpoints are placed at every irreversible-action boundary (sending an email, posting a journal entry, releasing a payment, submitting a regulatory filing, accepting a credit application). The model's output discipline matches the institution's existing-process output discipline (a model-drafted SAR narrative is reviewed against the same four-eyes standard as a human-drafted SAR narrative; a model-prepared 10-Q footnote is reviewed against the same SEC-disclosure standard as a human-prepared footnote). Disclosure to the consumer or customer of the model's involvement follows the institution's existing fair-dealing posture and the SEC 2026 examination priorities (AI-washing risk on the upside, lack-of-disclosure risk on the downside).

**Ongoing monitoring and validation.** Performance metrics are defined per use case before deployment and are tracked over time: false-positive and false-negative rates for screening surfaces, dispute rate for customer-facing surfaces, override rate for human-in-the-loop surfaces, hallucination rate for narrative surfaces, drift indicators (vocabulary drift, output-length drift, refusal-rate drift) for any surface running against a foundation model the vendor refreshes silently. Validation is independent of the model owner — an internal validation function, a second-line risk function, or an external validator — and is scoped to the tier. The validation cycle is documented (initial validation, change-driven re-validation, annual or more-frequent recurring validation depending on tier). Benchmark or challenger models, where the technology supports them, are operated alongside production for the highest-tier surfaces.

**Governance reporting and escalation.** A governance committee (typically the model risk committee, the AI governance committee, or a joint body) reviews the AI inventory, the validation results, the incident log, and the change pipeline on a cadence proportional to the institution's overall AI footprint. Material incidents — a sanctions miss, a SAR-narrative factual error that reached filing, a customer-decision outcome that produced fair-lending exposure, a payment-release that exceeded the mandate scope, a regulatory-filing line that triggered a comment letter — escalate to the institution's board-risk committee under existing escalation protocols, with the AI dimension explicitly called out so the board record reflects the AI-specific governance question. The forthcoming RFI is the supervisory signal that this discipline will be inspected; institutions that build the record now will have the audit trail to demonstrate the discipline.

## How This Composes Into KRASA Skills

The model-risk discipline composes into every KRASA skill that produces an output an institution acts on. Each skill operating in a regulated institution should expect to be:

- **Inventoried** — the skill is a model under the institution's definition; the skill's deployment context (Claude Cowork plugin / Claude Code / Managed Agent / firm-built wrapper) is part of the inventory entry; version pinning of the SKILL.md file is the model-version control
- **Tiered** — the skill's audience-template selection (per the v2.1 idiom — Daily Investigator Triage Queue vs. BSA-Officer Queue-Health Dashboard, for instance) and the output use (internal review draft vs. regulator-facing material vs. customer-facing communication) determine the tier
- **Validated** — the Anti-Plagiarism Note, the Personalization Hooks block, the Compliance Layer, and the Handoff Contracts block are validation-friendly surfaces that document what the skill does, the boundary conditions, the regulatory framing, and the input / output contracts
- **Monitored** — the skill's deployment should be monitored for the tier-appropriate metrics; high-tier deployments (AML case triage, sanctions screening, regulatory filing checks, credit covenant monitoring, ICFR controls assessment) should run with monitoring that detects drift in the underlying model, drift in skill outputs against historical patterns, and drift in human-override rates

The tier assignment is a use-context question, not a skill-design question.

## Implementation Cautions

**The exclusion is not a safe harbor.** Reading the agency text as "gen AI and agentic AI are unregulated" is wrong and dangerous. The exclusion is a *scope limitation on the new guidance text* — every other supervisory expectation (operational resilience, fair lending, BSA/AML, SOX §404, SEC Examination Priorities, OCC heightened standards, FRB SR letters on AI specifically, NYDFS Part 500 / Part 504, GLBA, CFPB enforcement posture) continues to apply. Institutions that read the exclusion as permission to defer governance are accumulating regulatory risk under existing supervisory frameworks while waiting for new guidance that may take twelve to twenty-four months to arrive.

**The RFI is the next regulatory signal.** Institutions should prepare an RFI-response posture in advance: which AI surfaces are in production, what discipline is documented, what would be costly under prescriptive text, what the institution would advocate for in supervisory text. The RFI window will be the institution's opportunity to shape the eventual guidance.

**Vendor model-risk is the institution's model-risk.** Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, AWS Bedrock, Cohere, Mistral, Meta and any other foundation-model provider sits behind the institution's agent surfaces. Vendor-side model changes (a silent Claude Opus version refresh, a tokenizer change, a refusal-policy tweak, a tool-use behavior shift) propagate to the institution's deployed agents. The institution's MRM discipline needs vendor-side change-monitoring: subscribe to the vendor's release notes, validate against the prior baseline after every announced model change, and treat unannounced output-distribution shifts as material change events.

**Subagent topology multiplies the validation surface.** A composite agent (the Anthropic May 5 pitch-builder, the FIS Financial Crimes AI Agent, the TradingAgents bull / bear / fund-manager topology, the Co-Investigator AI multi-specialist pattern, the BMAD-CORE™ multi-agent persona ensemble) is a composition of models. The validation discipline needs to test the composition end-to-end, not just the individual model components. The combinatorial space is large; the institution should prioritize the composition paths that touch high-tier outputs and accept that lower-tier path validation is sampled rather than exhaustive.

**Disclosure is a model-risk dimension.** SEC 2026 Examination Priorities make AI disclosure scrutiny explicit. A model that produces a customer-facing output, a Form ADV update, a Marketing Rule submission, a fund prospectus revision, a pillar-3 disclosure, or any other regulator-or-client-facing material brings disclosure exposure that the MRM discipline needs to recognize as a control gap if the model's role is not appropriately disclosed.

**Explainability is the credit-decisioning and AML-decisioning pressure point.** The OCC's 2026 supervisory posture on AI in credit decisioning, the FinCEN program-effectiveness rule direction in AML, and state-level fair-lending enforcement (the Massachusetts AG fair-lending settlement on AI underwriting, July 2025, as one early reference point) collectively make "black box" treatment of customer-decisioning models a heightened-risk posture. Skills that draft, support, or contribute to customer-decisioning outputs should be designed with the explainability dimension in mind — what evidence the model used, what reasoning chain produced the recommendation, what alternate dispositions were considered, what the human reviewer needs to validate.

**Records retention extends to model artifacts.** The model card, the SKILL.md file at the version in production, the prompt boundary, the validation report, the override log, and the monitoring artifact are records under the institution's recordkeeping discipline (SOX §802, SEC Rule 17a-4, Advisers Act Rule 204-2, FFIEC IT Handbook records-retention guidance, FDIC / OCC / Fed records-retention guidance). Treat them as financial records, not ephemeral application logs.
